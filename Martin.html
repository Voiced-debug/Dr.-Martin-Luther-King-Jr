Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.

Civil Right Activist and American Christain Minister

Facts about Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.

  • January 15, 1929 - Born Micheal King Jr. in Atlanta, Georgia
  • August 1934 - Was referred to as Micheal King Jr. Martin Luther King Jr.
  • May 18, 1941 - Maternal grandmother died after having snuck away to watch a parade
  • 1942- Aged 13, he became the youngest assistant manager of a delivery station for Atlanta Journal
  • April 13, 1944 - In his junior year, he delivered his first public speech during an oratorical contest sponsored by the Improved Benevolent and Protective Order of Elks of the World in Dublin, Georgia stating in his speech Black America still wears chains. The finest negro is at the mercy of the meanest white man.Even winners of our highest honors face the class color bar.
  • April 13, 1944 - Was made to stand along with his teacher on his way back home from the contest by the driver of the bus so white passengers could sit down as all the seat were taken. Later, he would write saying That night will never leave my memory. It was the angriest I have ever been in my life.
  • 1944 - Aged 15, he passed the entrance examination for Morehouse College and was enrolled for the school season that Autumn
  • 1947 - In his last year at Morehouse, 18 year old King choose to enter the ministry
  • 1951 - Graduated with a B.Div degree from Crozer Theological Seminary
  • 1951 - Began doctoral studies in Systematic Theology at Boston University
  • June 18, 1953 - Married Coretta Scott on the lawn of her parents' house in her hometown
  • 1954 - Aged 25, he was called as a pastor of the Dexter Avenue Baptist Church in Montgomery, Alabama
  • June 5, 1955 - Received his Ph.D degree with a dissertation titled A Comparison of the Conceptions of God in the Thinking of Paul Tiller and Nelson Wiemann
  • December 1, 1955 - Montgomery bus boycott which lasted for 385 days
  • July 23, 1957 - His birth certificate was altered to show his name as Martin Luther King Jr.
  • 1957 - Founded the Southern Christain Leadership Conference (SCLC) with Ralph Abernathy, Fred Shuttlesworth, Joseph Lowery and other civil right activists
  • 1957 - Received the Springarm Medal from the NAACP
  • September 20, 1958 - Was stabbed with a letter opener by Izola Curry while signing copies of his book Stride Toward Freedom in Blumstein's department store in Harlem
  • December 1959 - Announced his return to Atlanta at the request of the SCLC after five years in Montgomery
  • 1959 - Received the Ansfield-Wolf Book Award for his book Stride Towards Freedom: The Montgomery Story
  • May 4, 1960 - Was stopped while driving writer Lilian Smith to Emory University for driving without a license because he had not yet been issued a Georgia license
  • October 19, 1960 - Participated in a sit-in at the restaurant inside Ricks, Atlanta largest department store to desegregate businesses and public spaces in the city. He was among the many arrested
  • October 25, 1960 - Was sentenced to 4 months of hard labor by judge J. Oscar Mitchell
  • December 15, 1961 - He was involved in the Albany movement. He was arrested the next day
  • July 1962 - Returned to the movement , was given the option of 45 days in jail or a $178 fine. He choosed jail
  • 1962 - King and the Gandhi Society produced a document that called on the President to follow in the footsteps of Abraham Lincoln and issue an executive order to deliver a blow for civil rights as a kind of second emancipation proclamation
  • March 1963 - Marched on Washington where he gave a 17-minutes speech I have a Dream
  • April 1963 - The SCLC began a campaign against racial segregation and economic injustice in Birmingham, Alabama. He was arrested and jailed - his 13th arrest out of 29
  • 1963 - Time Person of the Year
  • February 6, 1964 - Delivered an inaugural speech of a lecture series initiated at the New School titled The American Race Crisis
  • December 1964 - King and the SCLC joined forces with the Student Non-violent Coordinating Committee in Selma, Alabama due to voter's registration
  • 1964 - Nobel Peace Prize
  • March 7, 1965 - First attempt to march to Montgomery from Selma. It later resulted to mob violence. This day is known as Bloody Sunday. King was not present
  • March 9, 1965 - Marched with others to Edmund Pethus bridge in Selma
  • March 11, 1965 - He cried as news of Johnson supporting a voting right bills on television in Marie Foster's living room
  • March 25, 1965 - Delivered a speech known as How Long, Not Long at the conclusion of the march
  • 1966 - Moved with Bevel and other civil right organisations to Chicago to demonstrate their support and empathy for the poor
  • 1966 - Planned Parenthood Federation of America awarded him the Margaret Sanger Award
  • 1966 - Elected as fellow of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences
  • April 4, 1967 - Delivered a speech title Beyond Vietnam: A time to break Silence at an appearance at the New York City Riverside Church against the U.S role in the Vietnam war
  • April 5, 1967 - Participated and spoke at the anti war march from Manhattan's Central park to the United Nations
  • November 1967 - Received an honorary degree from NewCastle University
  • 1968 - He and the SCLC organized the Poor People's Cammpaign to address issue of economic justice
  • March 29, 1968 - Went to Memphis, Tennessee in support of the black sanitary public works employees
  • April 3, 1968 - Addressed a rally and delivered his I've been to the Mountain Top addreaa at the Mason Temple, the world headquarters of the Church of God in Christ
  • April 4, 1968 - Fatally shot by James Earl at 6:01 PM as he stood on his motel's second floor balcony
  • April 4, 1968 - Died at St. Joseph's hospital at 7:05 PM
  • 1977 - Presidential Medal of Freedom awarded posthumously by President Jimmy Carter
  • 2004 - Congressional Gold Medal
  • April 20, 2016 - Images from King's I have a Dream speech was included in the reverse of the $5 bill

Here is the citation read at the event of presentation of his Presidential Medal of Freedom

Martin Luther King Jr. was the conscience of his generation. He gazed upon the great wall of segregation and saw that the power of love could bring it down. From the pain and exhaustion of his fight to fulfill the promises of our founding fathers for our humblest citizens, he wrung his eloquent statement of his dream for America. He made our nation stronger because he made it better. His dreams sustains us yet

-- President Jimmy Carter
